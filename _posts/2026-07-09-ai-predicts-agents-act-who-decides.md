Industrial AI is entering an era of unprecedented capability.

Large language models. Foundation models. Industrial agents. Optimization. Digital twins.

Yet despite these advances, one problem remains largely unsolved:

**How do we engineer industrial AI systems that people can trust to support — or execute — consequential operational decisions?**

<!--more-->

## The structural blind spot

The industry has organized itself around two halves of the problem.

**Prediction.** Solved, or solving fast. Models keep getting better. Inference keeps getting faster. Training infrastructure, transfer learning, foundation models — an enormous amount of capital and talent is pointed at this half.

**Execution.** Also solving fast. If you need to *do* something, there are agents. Orchestration frameworks. APIs. Workflow engines. Tool use.

But between prediction and execution sits an ungoverned middle — the decision layer.

The place where:
- A model outputs a number
- An algorithm interprets it
- A threshold fires
- Someone — or no one — decides if action is warranted
- Action gets taken

Almost nobody is asking: **who decided this?** And if they had to put their name on it, could they?

## Why this matters more in industrial environments

In enterprise software, this gap rarely matters. If a recommendation engine suggests the wrong product, the downside is a wasted ad impression.

Industrial AI is different. It touches:

- Safety
- Production continuity
- Asset integrity
- Environmental performance
- Human lives
- Billion-dollar equipment

In these environments, "the model was 90% accurate" is not a defense. The question that actually matters is: *who authorized this decision, with what authority, against what standard, and why did they believe it was safe?*

## This is the missing layer

Not a governance framework bolted on after the fact. Not a compliance checklist. An engineering discipline — the same way structural engineering, process safety, and reliability engineering are disciplines.

A governed decision layer grounds reasoning in engineering knowledge, makes the decision explicit and auditable, and gives a human or a system the standing to say: *I authorized this, and here is why.*

This is the first essay in a research program on engineering trust into industrial AI. Governance is one part of that larger discipline — the part this first volume is about.

---

**Who signs?** When a decision matters — when an engineer's judgment, a plant's safety, or a billion-dollar asset depends on it — can someone put their name on the decision your system just made?

That is where the real work begins.
